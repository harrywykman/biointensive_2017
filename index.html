What is Biointensive Gardening?

At its core, biointensive gardening is a closed gardening system that seeks to meet the needs of a human diet, without depleting soil.
Planning the garden is key to achieve both human nutrition and soil fertility.

Crops in Proportion

  • CARBON — 60%
    Over half of the area of a biointensive garden is devoted to growing crops that are high in carbon
  • CALORIES — 30%
    Nearly a third is grown to root crops that produce a high calorific value in a limited space
  • VITAMINS AND MINERALS — 10%
    One tenth is planted to vegetable crops that provide us with vitmains and minerals

How Much Land?

Why Biointensive?

The biointensive gardening method seeks to answer the question

"What would it take to feed the world on the smallest amount of land in perpetuity"

A it's core, the method aims to grow a human diet using a minimal land area whilst maintaining or increasing the capacity of the soil over time

What Draws me to this Method?

  1. Biointensive gardening addresses large scale issues of great concern
  2. It fits neatly into the Permaculture framework and is well combined with interests in regenerative farming, nutrient dense food and community gardening
  3. It provides a framework for living within ecological limits
  4. It is low tech and requires few resources, making it accessible to many people
  5. Allows us to take responsibility for our own part of the world and our own diet

Life within Ecological Limits

Biointensive gardening is planning and design intensive. It enables you to ascertain the diet you want to grow according to your nutritional need with how much space your diet would require. This allows us to take greater personal responsibility for what we consume.

How Much Land for A Diet?

0.2 ha / ≈ 0.5 acres
— arable land per head of global population {The United Nations Food and Agriculture Organisation 2009}

 

1.8 ha / ≈ 4.5 acres
— land per person with the biological capacity to produce goods to meet human needs (cropland, grazing land, forest, fishing ground and built land) {the Global Footprint Network (2007)}

 

100 m2 / ≈ 1000 ft2 / 0.01 ha / ≈ 0.025 acres
— area to meet all the nutritional needs of one person (~ 5% of FAO arable land allocation).  

400 m2 / ≈ 4000 ft2 / 0.04 ha / ≈ 0.1 acres
— area required to grow crops which leave woody (carbonaceous) material for compost (~ 20% of FAO arable land allocation).

Accessibility

The biointensive method is knowledge, design, skill and labour intensive.
This method is accessible to all people who have access to seed and people powered tools making it a system that the majority of the planet can use.
It has been taught successfully in over 140 countries. Ecology Action go to considerable efforts to train people from the developing world.

The Biointensive Gardening Method

Philosophy

The limits we have chosen for our work are that it must …

… be small scale, simple, manual and human.

… operate within a renewable resource consumption level, using an amount of resources available to each person in the world.

… include the growing of all our compost crops and nutrition on a closed system basis and the growing of a modest income as well.

… create, improve, and maintain sustainably a fertile, living soil, including the use of adequate amounts of humus (humidified carbon) from properly matured compost, so that the nutrients may be retained in the soil.

… be seen always as a whole way of life, a knowledge and skill to be learned over time and not just a series of techniques.

French Intensive Gardening

"In such a culture the primitive condition of the soil is of little account, because loam is made out of the old forcing beds … almost every plant is treated individually."

— Peter Kropotkin, Fields, Factories and Workshops of Tomorrow, p. 64

"In this culture each plant is treated according to its age. The seeds germinate and the seedlings develop their first four leaflets in especially favorable conditions of soil and temperature; then the best seedlings are picked out and transplanted into a bed of fine loam, under a frame or in the open air, where they freely develop their rootlets, and, gathered on a limited space, receive more than usual care. Only after this preliminary training are they bedded in the open ground, where they grow till ripe. In such a culture the primitive condition of the soil is of little account, because loam is made out of the old forcing beds … almost every plant is treated individually." — Peter Kropotkin, Fields, Factories and Workshops of Tomorrow

5. Calorie Growing

“… special calorie root crops, such as potatoes, sweet potatoes, leeks, onions, garlic, salsify, parsnips and Jerusalem artichokes, can enable us to grow, per unit of area, as much as five times, or more, the calories as can be produced with soybeans per unit of area and time.”

— John Jeavons p. 1 "An Experimental 33-Bed GROW BIOINTENSIVE® Mini-Farm: Growing Complete Fertility, Nutrition and Income"

  • Area Efficiency — a crop is considered to be "area efficient" if the annual area needed for total calories is less than or equal to 150 m2(≈ 1600 square feet)
  • Weight Efficiency — a crop is considered to be "weight efficient" if the daily weight of food required to be eaten for total calories is less than or equal to 4 kgs (≈ 9 lbs)

The Biointensive Framework as Design Tool

The biointensive research framework allows design questions to be posed and answered.

  • "What portion of my diet can I grow given the space I have available?"
  • "What set of crops will give me the most calories for area?"
  • "What set of crops will provide me the most critical nutrients for my diet?"
  • "How many plants of a particular crop will I need to fill 5 m2 of space?"
  • "How much space will 200 tomato plants take up at 60 cm spacing?"

A set of standard practices and parameters (eg. std. garden bed size; plant spacing etc.) facilitate the answering of these questions during a design phase.
